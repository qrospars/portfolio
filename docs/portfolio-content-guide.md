# Portfolio content and template guide

This is the mandatory reference for how Quentin Rospars's portfolio content is written, structured, illustrated, and reviewed. It applies to every public project, visual series, About-page change, caption, title, one-liner, metadata field, and CTA. AI agents must read this document and `AGENTS.md` before proposing or changing public content.

The portfolio should read as a personal story from a multidisciplinary maker, not a case-study template. Its voice is warm through real detail, thoughtful, and unhurried.

## Positioning hierarchy

Frame Quentin's work in this order: **people first, commercial outcomes second, then AI, data, product, research, and visual craft as the means to deliver both**. The approved positioning is: “I start with people, connect that to commercial outcomes, and use AI, data, and product craft to make it real.”

Do not reduce Quentin to a single Data & AI identity. When a project is about AI, lead with the user or business problem it helps solve, then explain the technology as part of a broader practice.

## Voice

- Write in first person. Use **I** for Quentin's work and **we** only for genuine team work.
- Lead with the interesting question, constraint, surprise, or tension, not a generic summary or “I built”.
- Earn warmth through specific facts. What broke, surprised Quentin, or constrained the work is more useful than an adjective.
- Use plain English. Avoid hype, press-release language, and AI-obvious phrases such as “Certainly” or “In today's fast-paced world”.
- Never use em dashes. Restructure the sentence or use a comma instead.
- Do not use words such as *impactful*, *meaningful*, *game-changer*, or *quick win*.
- Be honest about what was difficult and what Quentin would do differently.
- End forward-looking, with what the work unlocked, taught, or points to.

## Project structure

Every project follows this shape so the work reads as one practice.

### Title

Use one stable project or product name in title case, without punctuation or quotes. Use a noun phrase when the work has no product name, for example “Customer Journey Foundation”. Do not use a question or a broad category as the title. The title must be identical in all listings and on the detail page. Client names belong in metadata or a subtitle, never as a replacement title.

### One-liner

Write one plain present-tense sentence that says what the artifact is or does from the reader's point of view. It must be complete, non-imperative, and not a fragment. Prefer “An image-processing tool that predicts a drug's efficacy from muscle-fiber imagery” to an instruction such as “Explore the…”.

### Story

Write three to five short paragraphs, approximately one to two minutes to read:

1. The problem or question, and why it mattered.
2. Quentin's role, approach, key decision, or constraint.
3. What was hard or surprising.
4. The outcome and what it unlocked, or the learning when there is no clean metric.

Do not invent a result, a metric, a date, an outcome, or a degree of ownership. If the inputs are incomplete, ask Quentin before writing.

### Metadata

Use a consistent, scannable block where the facts are known:

- Year or range
- Role
- Type or disciplines
- Tools and stack
- Client or context
- Links, such as a live demo, repository, or write-up

Use the same title convention and metadata order across a project. Keep external links and credits in one predictable location. Finish a detail page with the next work link and a single contact prompt.

## Confidentiality

Commercial work is described by sector, not client name, unless Quentin has explicitly approved naming that client. Examples include “a global jewellery retailer” and “a Nordic fashion marketplace”. Public personal work and approved employer work may be named. Never publish budgets, contract terms, pricing, internal person names, or confidential implementation details. If a project cannot be described safely, omit it or ask for guidance.

## Visual and media standard

Each project should have:

- A strong hero image or a muted looping 5–12 second video with a still poster fallback.
- Two to four supporting visuals that advance the story, not decorative screenshots.
- At least one “it works” view showing the artifact doing its job.
- A concise, first-person caption and meaningful alt text for every visual.

Use consistent aspect ratios within a project, export images at suitable high density, and lazy-load media below the fold. Keep video compressed (MP4/WebM) and provide a still fallback. If raw material is unavailable, prefer one high-quality still or a clean re-recording of a public demo over a weak gallery. Do not use throwaway lead-ins such as “screenshots below”.

## Required inputs before drafting a new entry

Ask Quentin for any missing item. Do not infer it.

1. Project name and a plain one-sentence description.
2. Year or range and Quentin's specific role.
3. The problem or question.
4. The key approach, decision, or constraint.
5. What was difficult or surprising.
6. Outcome or learning.
7. Tools and stack.
8. Client/context and whether it must be anonymised.
9. Available media, live demo, or repository.

## Pre-handoff self-check

- Does it open with the interesting part, rather than “I built”?
- Is it first person, and is “we” used only for real team work?
- Is there an honest difficulty or surprise?
- Is the title stable, title case, and neither a question nor category?
- Is the one-liner complete, present tense, non-imperative, and error-free?
- Is the verified metadata complete and consistently ordered?
- Are client confidentiality and factual approval respected?
- Does every visual have both a caption and alt text, with a motion or “it works” visual planned where possible?
- Are there any em dashes, hype words, generic AI phrasing, invented facts, or unapproved names?
- Read it aloud. Does it sound personal and specific rather than templated?

## Relationship to existing content

Existing published copy remains intact unless Quentin explicitly requests a content rewrite. This guide governs future drafting and authorised revisions; it does not authorise changing historical claims, titles, dates, or project narratives by itself.
