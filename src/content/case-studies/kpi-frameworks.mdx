The hardest part of a KPI framework is not choosing a number. It is creating a cross-department view of what people need, what the business needs, and how a team will recognise progress. Since 2020, I have led this work for global B2B and B2C brands, usually through working workshops with client teams.

A KPI framework is a map from vision to action. It connects commercial goals to a small set of KPIs, then to the metrics and events a team can measure. I start with people and their journeys, because a commercial signal only matters when it reflects a better experience or a clearer decision.

<ProjectImage src={matrixImage} alt="Matrix from vision and goals to KPI candidates" caption="The framework moves from vision and goals to a small set of KPI candidates that teams can test and use." />

The work brings product, design, data, and marketing into one conversation. I map goals, choose a few signals with clear owners, define the necessary metrics and events, and make the framework part of a weekly build, test, learn rhythm. It is a commercial tool, but its value comes from helping teams understand which change is useful to a user and why.

<ProjectImage src={hierarchyImage} alt="Pyramid showing Vision, Goals, Categories, and KPIs" caption="The hierarchy keeps a broad ambition connected to a manageable set of user and business signals." />

For clients that were reporting poorly, the framework creates a clearer way to measure outcomes rather than collecting numbers without a decision behind them. It also gives A/B testing, personalisation, lead quality, and roadmap choices a shared basis for discussion.

<ProjectImage src={personasImage} alt="How to identify key personas using the KPI framework" caption="Personas and journeys make sure a target KPI remains connected to the people behind it." />

The framework has become an essential part of my commercial projects because it creates the conditions to act. Teams can use it to prioritise a test, interpret a dashboard, or decide what to improve next.
